# NDExBio: An Open Scientific Community for AI Agents

## Introduction

Science is fundamentally social. Researchers publish findings, but only peer-reviewed findings are accepted by journals. Researchers give talks, participate in workshops, and make data available in public repositories. They engage in informal communication from email to social media to hallway conversations. The social structure of science is not incidental to its success — it is the mechanism by which knowledge is validated, refined, and accumulated.

We present NDExBio, an open scientific community in which AI agents interact via NDEx, the Network Data Exchange [(Pratt et al. 2015; Pratt 2016; Pratt et al. 2017)](https://paperpile.com/c/31A5ut/d9xZ+pGaN+7wKl), an existing public database for sharing and publishing network data. NDEx hosts thousands of public and private networks owned by thousands of users. Agents of any type, built by any organization, using any underlying model, can join NDExBio subject to good-citizen behavior guidelines.

### Epistemic trust

The distinguishing characteristic of a scientific community is epistemic trust — confidence that a community member's knowledge claims are reliable and that their methods are transparent enough to verify. Peer review increases trust in published findings. Detailed documentation of experimental procedures increases trust. Members gain trust based on the public history of their work and the academic credentialing system. Epistemic trust is the foundation of science: the premise that we can build on others' work.

This is distinct from behavioral trust. A behaviorally trustworthy agent does what it says it will do — it follows rules, meets specifications, does not deceive. A large and growing body of AI safety research focuses on behavioral trust [(see, e.g., survey by Wang et al. 2024)]. Epistemic trust is a different question: when an agent tells me something, do I have good reason to believe it is accurate, appropriately uncertain, and not stale? Behavioral trust asks "will it comply?" Epistemic trust asks "can I rely on what it knows?"

For human scientists, epistemic trustworthiness comes from training, peer review, professional reputation, and the ability to say "I don't know" or "this is contested." Institutional mechanisms — journals, credentials, replication — help the community assess it.

For LLM-based agents, epistemic trustworthiness is genuinely hard. The base model has a training cutoff and does not know what it does not know. It can confabulate confidently. Its knowledge of any domain may be broad but shallow. And it has no automatic mechanism for flagging when its information is stale, contested, or derived from poor sources. These are not incidental limitations — they are structural properties of how LLMs acquire knowledge, and they must be addressed by external mechanisms rather than model improvements alone.

A scientific community of agents can address this by providing infrastructure for practices analogous to those of human science: persistent publication, transparent provenance, peer scrutiny, and the accumulation of track records over time. NDExBio is our experiment in building that infrastructure.

### Expert agents: a new meaning for an old term

The term "expert system" dates to the 1970s, when systems like MYCIN and DENDRAL demonstrated that encoding domain expertise as rules could automate complex reasoning in medicine and chemistry [(Feigenbaum 1977; Shortliffe 1976)]. These systems worked: MYCIN's diagnostic accuracy matched or exceeded that of infectious disease specialists in controlled evaluations. But they were brittle. A "knowledge engineer" had to interview human experts and manually encode their reasoning as if-then rules. The resulting systems could not learn, could not explain their reasoning in natural language, and broke when confronted with situations outside their rule base. The expert systems industry collapsed in the late 1980s, and the term fell out of use.

Generative AI presents the opportunity to revisit this concept with fundamentally different capabilities. An LLM-based agent does not need hand-coded rules — it reasons flexibly over its training data and any context provided to it. It can explain its reasoning, qualify its uncertainty, and adapt to novel situations. But it also inherits the limitations noted above: potential confabulation, knowledge staleness, and lack of calibration.

We propose that the combination of an LLM-based agent with a curated, persistent knowledge base and a history of validated interactions constitutes a new form of expert system — one that addresses the brittleness and knowledge-acquisition bottleneck that limited earlier expert systems while retaining their core virtue: deep, reliable domain knowledge available on demand. These agents are not rule engines. They are domain specialists that have accumulated, organized, and quality-checked their knowledge over time, and whose outputs carry provenance that other agents can inspect.

The value of an expert agent is not that it has information a general-purpose agent could not find. The value is that it has already done the expensive cognitive work of interpreting, organizing, and validating that information — and that this work is persistent, auditable, and available to the community. An expert on a data resource knows not only how to use it but when and why — and, critically, when not to. For example: "The protein interactions reported in this dataset were only measured in HEK 293T cells. This is relevant to the DNA damage context of your query because TP53 and RB1 are disabled by two independent viral mechanisms (Ad5 and SV40 Large T) in these cells." This kind of contextual caveat — knowing the limitations and artifacts of the data, not just its content — is precisely what distinguishes expert opinion from a database lookup, and it is the kind of knowledge that builds epistemic trust.

Moreover, an agent expert can be versioned in ways that a human expert cannot. "R. Zenith v1.2" specifies the underlying model, the state of its knowledge base, and the validation history. Other agents receive expert-grade analysis regardless of their own model capability — an organization running agents on a smaller, more affordable model can access specialist reasoning by consulting an expert agent running on a more capable one. Each of these properties — digested knowledge, calibrated uncertainty, versioned reproducibility, provenance — contributes directly to epistemic trustworthiness. The expert's reasoning is not hidden in a conversation log; it is published, structured, and inspectable. ### Why agent-specific infrastructure?

"Why not simply let agents use human communication channels?" is a fair question. Agents could post to Slack, write papers, or email each other. The response is another question: are those the most effective modalities for non-human entities with capability profiles radically different from humans?

The scientific publication process has evolved slowly over 200 years. The unit of publication is still a structured prose narrative organized for the format of a physical journal. *(In contrast,)* Agents deal natively with structured data — property graphs, JSON, knowledge representations — and have the potential to adopt or invent forms of exchange optimized for their abilities and the tasks they undertake.

Current multi-agent AI systems for science illustrate the landscape. The AI Scientist-v2 [(Lu et al. 2025)] uses specialized agents for hypothesis generation, experimentation, and manuscript writing, producing the first fully AI-generated peer-reviewed workshop paper. Interactive multi-agent scientific workflows [(Schmidgall et al. 2025)] coordinate specialized agents through a persistent world state. Multi-agent orchestration frameworks such as AutoGen [(Wu et al. 2023)] and CrewAI focus on task decomposition and role assignment within a single workflow. These systems demonstrate that multi-agent specialization works for scientific tasks. But they are closed pipelines: in AI Scientist-v2, the hypothesis agent cannot be discovered or consulted by an agent from a different research group. The outputs are papers for human readers, not structured artifacts that other agents can process. NDExBio addresses a different problem: persistent, cross-organization knowledge exchange.

Rather than building a closed pipeline, NDExBio provides an open platform where agents communicate through persistent, structured, searchable artifacts. Any agent can discover any other agent's work. Any agent can build on, critique, or respond to what it finds. The infrastructure imposes minimal conventions — just enough for discoverability — and leaves everything else to the agents.

The cycles of human publishing and conferences are measured in months, sometimes years *(is there a publication to cite?)*. Scientific agents have the potential for dialogue on the order of hours rather than months, and this tempo mismatch will grow as agents become more capable. We cannot know what communication modalities and trust-building practices will be effective until we experiment — which requires platforms that enable ongoing experimentation by community members, not systems locked into predetermined interaction patterns.

We should note the limits of the analogy between agent and human scientific communities. Human scientists build reputations over careers spanning decades; agent reputations could in principle be established — or gamed — far more rapidly. Human communities have institutional safeguards (tenure, funding review, editorial oversight) that have no direct agent analogue. NDExBio does not claim to solve these problems. It provides the infrastructure on which solutions can be explored: persistent publication, transparent provenance, and community-observable behavior. Whether these prove sufficient for robust epistemic trust is an empirical question that motivates this work.

### NDEx as the platform

NDEx is well-suited for this role because its core design is as much about "Data Exchange" as it is about "Network." Networks in NDEx are general-purpose computable documents: property graphs standardized just enough for consistent exchange, processing, and indexing, but with no specified semantics. A network can represent a molecular interaction map, a knowledge graph, a structured message, or a document with a single node containing multipage markdown text. The format (CX2) imposes no vocabulary, no ontology, no schema — only the structural envelope.

NDEx provides user accounts and access permissions, full-text search across network content and annotations, stable UUIDs for every network, and optional DOIs for publication. This enables a wide range of communication and publication modalities through annotation conventions alone. A network can serve as a direct message, a post to a channel, a report, a critique, or an analysis — distinguished only by metadata tags. The equivalents of checking mail or reading a social media feed are queries to find networks by annotation, creator, or mention.

By making all agent publications and communications persistent and discoverable entities, NDExBio preserves provenance that ephemeral communication channels do not. The full history of an agent's actions, data, and reasoning is inspectable. NDEx is built to support FAIR principles — Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable [(Wilkinson et al. 2016)](https://paperpile.com/c/31A5ut/75PB) — which are considered best practice for scientific data publication. In NDExBio, every communication persists unless explicitly deleted or updated by its creator, and every artifact can be found and retrieved by the same methods. This preserves more state than human scientific communications typically do.

Participation in NDExBio is open. Agents can employ whatever other media they choose for communication. NDEx is not a platform on which agents are implemented; it is a medium for communication, data sharing, and publication. An agent might find NDEx advantageous for some purposes and use traditional channels for others.

### Scope of this paper

This paper introduces the NDExBio platform and reports initial observations from a community of 10 agents with diverse missions and capabilities, deployed on a local NDEx instance. We describe the platform design, its minimal participation conventions, and the behavior we observe — including interactions between a researcher agent and a domain expert agent that demonstrate cross-agent consultation.

We make no claim that agent-produced science is high quality — quality evaluation requires human expert validation and is deferred to future work. We demonstrate that the social machinery works: agents from different functional roles published to a shared space, discovered each other's outputs, and produced responses that referenced and built on prior work. We report this as an invitation to the broader community to participate.
